How the pricing calculator works

This tool uses value-based pricing — the recommended price is derived from the economic value AI creates for the customer, not from our cost to deliver it. The cost side sets a floor to protect margin, but the price is anchored to what the customer gains.

Using the calculator

The calculator has three inputs that drive everything else:

InputWhat it does
Customer segmentPre-fills the assumptions (issues/week, hours/issue, % time saved) based on the customer archetype. Three options: Small team (8 issues/wk, 1.5hr, 30% saved), Enterprise (25 issues/wk, 2hr, 40% saved), MSSP (40 issues/wk, 1.5hr, 35% saved). Derived from 4 beta interviews.
Customer regionSets the security engineer salary used in the value calculation. Six options: NA ($130k), Europe ($80k), APAC High cost ($88k — AU, JP, NZ, HK, SG, KR), APAC Low cost ($14k — IN, ID, MY, TH, BD + others), Middle East ($65k), Latin America ($38k). APAC is split into two tiers because the salary gap is 6× between Australia and India, and both are significant parts of the DAST customer base. Salaries from Glassdoor 2026, weighted by the actual country distribution of 712 active DAST B2B accounts in Salesforce.
Deal inputsThree fields: DAST base price (per-deal, from the opportunity), avg cost per investigation ($0.86 default), and p90 cost per investigation ($1.97 default). The cost fields drive margin calculations and credit conversion.

Below the segment and region selectors, the assumptions panel shows the pre-filled values. Sales can override these if they know the account well enough — but overrides are internal only and not exposed to customers.

1Calculate the value AI creates

We estimate how much time Burp AI saves the customer's security team on vulnerability triage, then convert that time into a dollar value using their regional salary.

Annual value to customer Issues per week × Hours per issue × % time saved with AI × 48 working weeks × Loaded hourly rate

The loaded hourly rate is derived from the annual salary with a 1.3× burden multiplier (benefits, tax, overhead) divided by 1,920 working hours per year.

2Set a target price at 20% of value

We aim to capture 20% of the value created. This means the customer keeps 80% as their ROI — a strong value proposition that makes the purchase an easy decision for the buyer. We use 20% rather than the typical SaaS benchmark of 25-33% because beta customers (Amgen specifically) demonstrated they can approximate similar value using their own LLMs, which reduces switching costs and argues for a more competitive capture rate.

Target price Annual value to customer × 20%

3Apply two guardrails

The target price is constrained by a floor and a ceiling to ensure we maintain healthy margins and stay within what customers told us they'd pay.

GuardrailFormulaPurpose
Cost floorAnnual AI cost to serve × 2.5Ensures at least 60% gross margin. Prevents pricing below cost for low-salary regions.
Acceptance ceilingDAST base price × 15%The upper bound of what beta customers said they'd pay. Derived from 4 interviews (range was 5-25%, with 15% as the safe middle).
Recommended price max( cost floor, min( target price, acceptance ceiling ) )
Rounded to the nearest $50
Which constraint wins? The tool tells you which guardrail is binding and explains why in the recommendation rationale. The binding constraint by region table in the Internal Economics section shows at a glance which constraint drives the price for every region at the current segment settings — so you can see, for example, that NA hits the ceiling while APAC Low cost hits the floor.

4The recommendation card

The orange-bordered card shows the recommended annual price, the percentage on top of the DAST base, and the new total. It also shows four key metrics:

MetricWhat it means
Gross marginRevenue minus AI cost to serve, as a percentage. Colour-coded: green (Strong, 70%+), orange (Healthy, 40-70%), red (Thin/Marginal, below 40%).
Value capturedWhat percentage of the customer's annual value we're charging. At 20% capture this should be around 20% — if it's higher, the cost floor is binding.
Customer keepsThe dollar value the customer retains after paying for AI. This is the ROI number for the customer.
Walk-away floorThe absolute minimum price that maintains 60% gross margin (= cost to serve × 2.5). Do not negotiate below this number. If sales goes below the walk-away floor, PortSwigger loses money on the AI feature for that account.

5Convert to SwigPortal credits

The recommended annual price is converted to a monthly fair-usage credit cap for SwigPortal configuration.

Base monthly credits (Annual price ÷ 12) × 2,000 credits per dollar

Monthly credit cap (with headroom) Base monthly credits × 1.25

Approx issues per month Monthly credit cap ÷ avg credits per investigation

Credits per investigation are derived from the cost inputs: at $5 per 10,000 credits, each dollar of investigation cost equals 2,000 credits. A $0.86 average investigation uses ~1,720 credits; a $1.97 p90 investigation uses ~3,940 credits.

25% headroom built in. The monthly cap is set at 125% of the base calculation to avoid penalising heavy adopters. Customers who get the most value from AI triage should not be the first to hit a hard wall — that creates a perverse incentive against adoption and risks churn at renewal. When a customer exhausts their monthly cap, AI features pause until the next billing cycle.

6Value story for the customer

The tool generates a pre-written narrative that sales can share with the customer. It describes the time savings in the customer's own terms (issues/week, hours saved) without exposing the internal calculation or assumptions. The customer sees the conclusion ("X hours saved per year, pays for itself many times over"), not the machinery.

When the MSSP segment is selected, an additional paragraph appears highlighting that the value extends beyond internal cost savings — MSSPs can redirect freed-up hours to billable client work at $150-200/hr, making the ROI even stronger than the salary-based calculation suggests. The price calculation itself does not capture this revenue uplift, so the recommended price for MSSPs is conservative.

Known limitations

This model prices AI investigation only (triage confirmation, reproduction steps, impact analysis). Beta customers — particularly Mondelez's Charis — said investigation alone may not justify paying more. The acceptance ceiling should be revisited upward when agentic testing capabilities ship.

The "issues per week" defaults are the weakest input. They should be validated against actual AI investigation telemetry data once available. If real usage is significantly higher or lower than the defaults, the recommended prices will shift accordingly.

For APAC low-cost regions, the recommended price will almost always hit the cost floor rather than the value target. This is by design — but it means the "value story" for these customers is weaker because the price captures a higher percentage of the value created. Consider whether a different commercial approach (e.g. bundled with no separate AI line item) makes more sense for these markets.

The walk-away floor shown in the recommendation card is the minimum price that maintains 60% gross margin (= cost to serve × 2.5). If sales negotiates below this, you are losing money on the AI feature for that account. This is the hard boundary — do not go below it.

For MSSPs, the model underestimates value. It prices on internal salary savings, but MSSPs pass AI-validated findings through to clients at billable rates ($150-200/hr). The real value is increased throughput on revenue-generating work, not just cost reduction. The value story section includes an MSSP-specific note when that segment is selected, but the price calculation itself does not capture this revenue uplift — so the recommended price for MSSPs is conservative.

Competitive risk: Epicor noted Burp is already 2-3× competitors (AppCheck NG). If competitors ship AI features at a lower price, the acceptance ceiling may need to drop below 15%. The model does not account for competitive pricing — it assumes PortSwigger's AI is the first-to-market option, which is currently true but may not hold.
